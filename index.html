About Me

Hello! I am Alonso, a computer science student currently attending the Illinois Institute of Technology in Chicago. In high school, I joined the robotics team which sparked my passion for programming.

I love programming robots and designing games. Being able to write a few lines of code on the computer and making an impact in the real world is something I find truly fascinating. Whether that is making a robot move and follow commands or making a game for others to enjoy.

The unquantifiable amount of joy and happiness you feel when the robot you've been programming for months executes its task during a competition to score those winning points or when other students you don't know come in, asking to play your game is the reason I love programming. Knowing that in the end, a difference will be made and that justifies all the hard work and effort you put into it.

Don't Stop Moving

Don't Stop Moving is the very first game I ever programmed. The premise of the game was that you are a test subject in an experimental facility. You have to escape the facility by collecting the fragments of a key while avoiding the facility's security measures. If you stop moving you die.

Below is the trailer I made for the game.

Everything had to be created by yourself for this game, art assets, sound effects, and the code. I had 3 weeks to make the whole game. This project allowed me to apply the knowledge I have learned on the use of variables, functions, conditionals and classes in java. Also, used more abstract concepts like finite state machines.

ZPG

“Earth in the future has a competition to keep the population under control, 2 people compete against each other through several tests, the competitor with the most victories, survives.”

That is the idea behind ZPG. The name is the acronym for Zero Growth Population. In this game you are competing against another player. There are 4 levels in this game. Each level tests a survival skill, speed, accuracy, resourcefulness, and defensive capabilities.

This game was more complex, incorporating arrays and arraylists combined with for loops to be able to organize and manipulate the data for the robots, targets and bullets.

Asteroids

This is my recreation of the 1979 classic arcade game of the same name.

Our assignment was to recreate a classic arcade game. I chose this game because it didn't use 4 way or 8 way movement. It used rotation and a value for speed to travel in that chosen angle.

While the gameplay is quite simple, I had to use some new techniques to make it happen. Angles and trigonemetry had to be used to set the speed on the x and y plane to the correct values to travel on the specified angle. It also had to be used for the targetting of the alien spaceship that shoots at the player. This is also the first time I used velocity and acceleration to have the player ramp up to a speed instead of being set instantaneously. There is also momentum that keeps the player drifting in the direction they last moved in to simulate the near-frictionless vacuum of space.

AVALANCHE!

AVALANCHE! In this game you have to steer a tumbling snowball out of harm's way to the bottom of the mountain.

This game was made for mobile devices. It uses the sensors in a phone to detect the tilt of it and that is used to control the snowball. It also uses the touch sensors to detect a tap to start the game and swipes for getting people out of the way of the snowball.

The challenge of the game comes from the growing size of the snowball. Its grows larger the longer it rolls on for making it more difficult to manuever in between tents.

I had to implement and use a third party library, Ketai, for the sensors and touch detection. This game was also structured differently to my previous games. I used switch cases to changed between levels instead of an if else chain. I also used inheritance to create more generalized parent classes since the obstacles and levels child classes shared many data. It was more efficient requiring less lines of code than my previous method.
